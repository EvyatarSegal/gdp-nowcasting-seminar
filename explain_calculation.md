Executive Methodology Report: The Economics Behind the AI

1. Introduction

In our institutional nowcasting pipeline, our final GDP prediction is generated by a "best of both worlds" ensemble:

A Dynamic Factor Model (DFM): A traditional macroeconomic model that is excellent at finding the hidden "heartbeat" of the economy.

Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost): An advanced Artificial Intelligence (AI) model that is excellent at finding complex, non-linear patterns.

While this AI-driven approach provides superior forecasting accuracy, it creates a transparency problem: XGBoost operates on the DFM's abstract "factors" rather than the raw data, rendering the original 51 macroeconomic variables (like retail sales or tech stocks) opaque.

To provide actionable, transparent insights to our shareholders and stakeholders, we must attribute the final ensemble forecast back to the original variables. We do this using a cutting-edge blend of Game Theory (SHAP) and Linear Algebra Projections. This report explains how we look inside the "black box."

2. Demystifying the Models: How Do They Think?

Before we calculate the final impact of any single variable, it helps to understand how our two models view the economy.

2.1 The DFM: Factors and Loadings

Imagine listening to a 51-piece orchestra. You can't listen to every single instrument perfectly at once, but you can easily hear the overall tempo and melody.

The DFM treats our 51 macroeconomic variables as the musicians, and it extracts the underlying "tempo" of the economy into 4 unobserved Latent Factors ($F$).

To calculate this, the DFM uses the Observation Equation:


$$X_{t} = C F_{t} + e_{t}$$


(Plain English: The actual data ($X$) we see today is driven by the hidden economic trend ($F$) multiplied by a specific weight ($C$), plus some random noise ($e$).)

The matrix $C$ is incredibly important. It contains the Factor Loadings. You can think of a loading as a variable's "sensitivity" to the underlying economy. If the economy surges, a variable with a high loading will surge dramatically, while a variable with a low loading will barely move. The model mathematically calculates these loadings by finding the principal vectors that explain the maximum variance in our historical dataset.

2.2 XGBoost and SHAP Values

XGBoost is our AI model. It takes the 4 economic factors generated by the DFM and uses thousands of decision trees to predict GDP. But how do we know which of the 4 factors actually drove the AI's final decision?

We use SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations). Derived from cooperative game theory (and winning its creator a Nobel Prize in Economics), SHAP calculates the fairest way to distribute credit among a team.

Imagine the 4 economic factors are players on a basketball team, and the team scores 100 points (the GDP forecast). Did Player A score the points, or did Player A only score because Player B passed the ball? SHAP answers this by calculating the model's prediction for every possible combination of players (e.g., A alone, A+B, B+C+D, etc.) to isolate the true, standalone marginal contribution of each factor.

The SHAP formula for a specific factor $i$ is:


$$\phi_i = \sum_{S \subseteq N \setminus \{i\}} \frac{|S|! (|N| - |S| - 1)!}{|N|!} [f(S \cup \{i\}) - f(S)]$$


(Plain English: The impact of Factor $i$ is the weighted average of how much the GDP forecast improves every time we add Factor $i$ to a theoretical scenario.)

3. Phase A: Calculating the Factor-Level "Pie"

Now that we understand the tools, we can begin attributing the current month's GDP forecast. First, we determine exactly how much each of the 4 factors shifted the GDP forecast by blending the "opinions" of both models using our predefined ensemble weights ($w_{xgb} = 0.60, w_{dfm} = 0.40$).

XGBoost Impact: We extract the SHAP values ($\phi$) from the AI. This tells us exactly how much Factor $k$ pushed the decision trees away from their baseline prediction.

DFM Impact: We calculate the traditional linear contribution of Factor $k$ by multiplying its current value ($F_k$) by its GDP loading ($C_{GDP, k}$) and scaling it by the historical GDP standard deviation ($\sigma_{GDP}$).

For each Factor $k$, the total combined impact is:


$$Impact_{k} = (0.60 \times SHAP_{k}) + (0.40 \times DFM\_Impact_{k})$$

Think of $Impact_{k}$ as the "pie." We know how large the pie is; now we must slice it up and hand those slices back to the original 51 variables.

4. Phase B: Slicing the Pie (The Linear Algebra Projection)

Because the 4 factors were mathematically constructed from the 51 variables, we can reverse-engineer the relationship.

To map the factors back to the variables perfectly, we use the DFM's Loading Matrix ($C$) to calculate a Projection Matrix ($W$) using the Generalized Least Squares formula:


$$W = (C'C)^{-1} C'$$

$C$ is a matrix of size $51 \times 4$ (Variables $\times$ Factors).

$W$ is a matrix of size $4 \times 51$ (Factors $\times$ Variables).

The matrix $W$ provides the exact mathematical map required to perfectly trace the factors back to their root variables.

5. Phase C: Applying Today's Data

A naive attribution model applies static historical rules. Our pipeline is dynamic: it evaluates the actual performance of the variables for the current month to ensure mathematical perfection.

Let $X_{current}$ be the actual data for our 51 variables this month, standardized so we can compare apples to apples.

For a specific Factor $k$, we calculate the Raw Variable Contribution for Variable $j$ by multiplying its projection weight by its actual performance today:


$$Raw\_Contribution_{j, k} = W_{k, j} \times X_{current, j}$$

Why is this important? If a stock market variable is historically very important (high weight $W$), but it traded completely flat today ($X = 0$), its contribution to today's GDP movement correctly drops to zero.

Next, we normalize these raw contributions so that they represent a percentage share, summing to exactly 1.0 (100% of the factor's movement):


$$Relative\_Weight_{j, k} = \frac{Raw\_Contribution_{j, k}}{\sum_{i=1}^{51} Raw\_Contribution_{i, k}}$$

6. Phase D: The Final Calculation

Finally, we multiply the variable's Relative Weight (Phase C) by the total Ensemble Factor Impact (the "Pie" from Phase A).

$$Final\_Impact_{j, k} = Relative\_Weight_{j, k} \times Impact_{k}$$

We repeat this process across all 4 factors. The total individual impact for a specific variable on the current GDP forecast is simply the sum of its impacts across all factors:


$$Total\_Impact_{j} = \sum_{k=1}^{4} Final\_Impact_{j, k}$$

The Integrity Test (Proof of Math)

Because the Relative Weights sum exactly to 1.0, and the Projection Matrix evaluates the actual real-world data $X$ for this exact month, there is zero mathematical leakage.

$$\sum_{j=1}^{51} Total\_Impact_{j} \equiv \sum_{k=1}^{4} Impact_{k}$$

The sum of the 51 variable contributions perfectly matches the sum of the factor impacts. This guarantees that when we present our findings to stakeholders, we can accurately and defensively state: "The Nasdaq index is strictly responsible for $+0.05\%$ of our current GDP growth nowcast."
